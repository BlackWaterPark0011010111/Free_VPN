servers ain't free, bandwidth costs money - there's NO such thing as free servers in this world, NO free IP addresses either. So when someone's offering you a "free" VPN service, you better believe they paid for it somehow (company money or whatever) and they gotta make that cash back somehow. That "free" VPN you just installed? Yeah it's packaging up all your data, tracking where you go online and at the very minimum selling that sweet intel to advertisers. Whoever runs the server calls the shots - they're holding all the cards. Now don't get me wrong, using a VPN is great for hiding your traffic from your ISP - they won't know what sites you visit or what you're doing. A good encrypted messenger takes it up another notch with that end-to-end encryption where your convos stay locked tight. But here's the kicker - that VPN claiming "we don't keep logs"? Total BS. They're not just logging, they're straight up auctioning off your IP to the highest bidder. Advertisers are one thing, but when three-letter agencies come knocking with a subpoena? Game over. You think you're slick with your free VPN all protected? Nah homie - your whole digital life is already sitting in a neat little folder with your picture on it. And if you're some special snowflake? Oh you're screwed - your digital footprint stands out like an elephant ice skating. Even paid "no logs" VPNs? If their servers are in countries that gotta hand over data when Uncle Sam comes calling, what's the damn point?
Again, who owns the company? Maybe it’s just a shell corporation waiting for you to start moving something serious through their server? For 100% anonymity with full confidence in your safety—that doesn’t exist. There are always holes you haven’t thought of. With a free VPN, you are the product; with a paid one, you need to check who’s behind it. Here, it’s either offline or knowing your stuff. And the internet—it’s always against you. But if you have a VPN and E2E, then the internet isn’t a pipe where you’re flying like a bullet through hmmmmm... It’s more like a dirty pond where every step you take leaves ripples in the water. Your browser is a snitch. Even if you’re using a VPN, all cookies (those little text files that websites leave in your browser to remember you—for example, session cookies store your logins or shopping cart data while you’re on the site, while persistent cookies remain after closing the browser, like saved passwords, so you don’t have to re-enter them, but they still stay there). And, by the way, even with a VPN—if you log into your own account, the cookies will link your identity to your new IP. Plus, there are trackers (those scripts that collect data on your behavior: they monitor what you look at, where you click—basically, the site owner adds a script to the HTML code of the page. When the tracker loads upon entering the site, your browser fetches this script (the tracker), and the surveillance begins: tracking page views, which buttons you press, how long you spend on the site, and much more). So yeah, it’s a FFFull-on scan.Then all the collected data gets sent to Facebook’s servers—fuck, any servers, really. Then the site grabs cookies and browser identifiers to link them to a specific user. And if you’re logged in—all those parameters get tied to your account, and then they show you ads for the shit you’ve been looking at lately. There are also social buttons for you, like "Share on Instagram" (meaning we embed code in the buttons, and even if you don’t click it, your browser still loads the script ‘cause the button’s already displayed—that’s enough). They snag the IP of whoever visited the site, plus cookie data (and again, if you’re logged in—everything gets linked to your profile). We pull data from social media servers and log that you were on the site. And VPN won’t save you, ‘cause if you logged into Google without a VPN, then turned it on later, trackers will link your old and new sessions.Fingerprinting ("hardware + software")—that’s their puzzle made of dozens of your browser and device parameters. It’s your screen resolution (if it’s rare—it tightens the suspect pool like your asshole clenches, kinda), your installed fonts, extensions, and all sorts of plugins, your OS version (like, the combo Win 11 + Chrome 12 + Russian language—that’s already unique). And even with a VPN, fingerprinting IDs you 99% of the time, just ‘cause you don’t change those settings. So they devour your anonymity like starving dogs. You visit a site—and it’s already figured you out by your digital fingerprint.You can’t hide shit if your screen is 1920×1080, your fonts are some quirky ones, and your plugins—if you’ve got a rare combo of settings—you stick out like a sore thumb.So, you go to a fishing forum with one IP, then hop onto some obscure anime site with a VPN—still screwed. They can use trackers from logged-in sites and figure out it’s the same person. You’re a damn unique snowflake.A free VPN isn’t a service—it’s a data-harvesting tool, and they make money off you in three ways:First—selling your traffic to advertisers. Every site you visit, every search query, every second you linger on a page—all that gets neatly packed into your digital profile and auctioned off. You’re just moving from a glass cage to one with a one-way mirror. Sure, your ISP can’t see you now—but the VPN provider’s watching you point-blank, along with everyone they sell your data to.Second—straight-up cybercrime. A ton of free VPNs are just wrappers for trojans and keyloggers. You install the client and get a bonus program that logs all your passwords—even if you type them in a secure messenger—or uses your PC to mine crypto (that’s why your laptop’s suddenly hot as an iron). It also turns your device into a botnet zombie for DDoS attacks. And when you uninstall the VPN, its little "gifts" stay behind—hidden services, scheduled tasks, startup modules.Third—traffic hijacking. The second you turn on the VPN, you hand over all your traffic and bandwidth to them. They feed you fake bank and social media sites that look legit, but your data goes straight to scammers. They inject ads and crypto miners into pages that never had ads, reroute your traffic through their proxies—and boom, your IP’s now part of some illegal scheme.Oh, and they can:Leak your real IP through WebRTC (especially if your browser isn’t configured right),send DNS requests outside the VPN (your ISP sees everything),ignore IPv6 (so your location gets exposed).
If you downloaded Tails or Whonix—awesome stuff, everything runs in RAM and leaves zero traces!
But then you go online from the same laptop under your real name—and boom, anonymity goes down the drain. Because your hardware is still the same, and even if you were "clean" in Tails but boot into regular Windows afterward—your hard drive, network card’s MAC address, BIOS, your anonymous Tor IP, and spoofed MAC—all of that can leak your identity completely.
Tor? Now that’s an even funnier story. Yeah, your traffic hops through three nodes—entry, middle, and exit. But if the exit node (the one that spits your traffic into the regular internet) is compromised (and they often are, controlled by either law enforcement or hackers)—well… anything you send in plaintext isn’t yours anymore. Forgot HTTPS? Congrats, your login and password are now in someone’s database. And then there’s metadata—it’s like the envelope of a letter: you can encrypt the text, but who sent it, who received it, and when—that’s visible to everyone, and Tor doesn’t hide connection times, data volume, or other related details.
